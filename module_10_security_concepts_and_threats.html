Lesson 1: Data & Information as an Asset

Core Principle: Data is one of the most valuable assets an organization possesses. Companies monetize data by improving products, targeting customers, or selling datasets. Protecting data according to its criticality is essential.

📋 Scenario

You're a new GRC analyst at a mid-size e-commerce company. During your first week, the CISO asks you to classify datasets across the organization. Some data drives daily revenue, while other data is archived and rarely accessed. How do you determine what gets the highest level of protection?

How Companies Monetize Data

💡 Improve Products

Companies analyze usage data to enhance features and fix problems.

Example: A streaming service tracks what users watch to recommend better content.

🎯 Target Customers

Behavioral data helps companies reach the right audience with marketing.

Example: An online retailer uses browsing history to show personalized advertisements.

💰 Sell Data

Some companies' entire revenue model is built on collecting and selling data to third parties.

Example: A data broker aggregates consumer profiles and sells them to advertisers.

Critical vs. Noncritical Data

🔴 Critical Data

Definition: Data that cannot be lost and requires the highest level of protection.

• Customer financial records • Medical patient data • Encryption keys • Trade secrets • Authentication databases

🟡 Noncritical Data

Definition: Data that can have less rigorous security standards without major business impact.

• Public marketing materials • Archived press releases • General website content • Non-sensitive internal memos • Publicly available reports

Core Principle: Nonrepudiation means that a party cannot credibly deny having performed an action. It provides proof that an event occurred and identifies the responsible party in a way that cannot be refuted. Video recordings, biometrics, digital signatures, and receipts are all mechanisms used to achieve nonrepudiation.

📋 Scenario

A vendor disputes a $940,000 contract, claiming they never signed it. An employee insists they never sent the email that approved a fraudulent payment. A customer denies ever completing a purchase. In each case, the organization needs to prove — beyond reasonable doubt — that these actions occurred and who was responsible. This is the problem nonrepudiation solves. What forms of evidence hold up?

Nonrepudiation Evidence Types

📹

Video Recording

CCTV, body cameras, and security footage provide visual proof that a person was at a specific place and time.

Use case: Proves an employee was in the server room at 2 AM when unauthorized changes occurred
★★★★★
👆

Biometrics

Fingerprints, iris scans, and facial recognition uniquely tie an action to a specific biological individual — not just a credential.

Use case: Fingerprint scanner at server room door proves which individual gained access
★★★★★
✍️

Digital Signatures

A cryptographic signature created with a user's private key mathematically proves that a specific individual signed or sent a document — it cannot be forged.

Use case: Contract e-signed with PKI certificate proves vendor agreed to all terms
★★★★★
🧾

Receipts & Transaction Records

Timestamped, system-generated records of completed transactions — purchases, transfers, approvals — that confirm an action occurred.

Use case: Payment receipt with timestamp proves the customer completed checkout
★★★★★
🖊️

Physical Signatures

Handwritten signatures on contracts, forms, or documents have been legally accepted evidence of agreement for centuries.

Use case: Signed delivery receipt proves the package was accepted by the recipient
★★★★★
🔐

Audit Logs & Timestamps

Tamper-proof logs with cryptographic timestamps prove that specific actions occurred at specific times by specific accounts.

Use case: SIEM log with hash chain proves the record has not been altered
★★★★★

★ Strength rating: 5 stars = cryptographically irrefutable; 3 stars = strong but potentially contestable in court

🔏 How Digital Signatures Provide Nonrepudiation

1. SIGN

Sender encrypts document hash with their private key → Digital signature created

→

2. SEND

Signed document + certificate transmitted to recipient

→

3. VERIFY

Recipient decrypts signature with sender's public key → Identity + integrity confirmed

Why it works: Only the sender possesses their private key. If the signature verifies correctly using the sender's public key, it is mathematically proven that the sender — and only the sender — created that signature. They cannot later deny it.

✅ Concept Check

Question: Which of the following BEST provides nonrepudiation for an electronically signed legal document?

A) A username and password used to log into the signing platform
B) An email from the signer's account confirming they "looked at" the document
C) A digital signature created with the signer's private key, verified by a trusted Certificate Authority
D) A witness who saw the person use the computer at the time
Explanation: A digital signature created with the signer's private key — and verified by a Certificate Authority — provides the strongest nonrepudiation because: (1) Only the signer possesses their private key, (2) the signature mathematically binds the signer's identity to the document, (3) any alteration to the document invalidates the signature, and (4) a trusted third party (CA) vouches for the identity. Passwords can be shared, emails can be spoofed, and witnesses can be discredited. A cryptographic digital signature cannot be forged.
