Lesson 7: Wired Networking and Telephone Connectors

Network Connection Types: Wired networks use RJ45 (Ethernet) connectors for twisted-pair copper cables, and SFP (Small Form-Factor Pluggable) connectors for fiber-optic cables. Telephone modems use RJ11 connectors. Understanding these connectors is essential for network troubleshooting.

📋 Scenario

A small business owner accidentally plugged their telephone cable into their computer's Ethernet port and can't get online. Meanwhile, IT is installing fiber connections in the server room. You need to explain the different network and telephone connectors to prevent confusion.

Network and Telephone Connector Comparison

🔌 RJ45 (Ethernet Connector)

Use: Twisted-pair copper network cables

  • Pins: 8 pins (8P8C modular connector)
  • Cable Type: Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a, Cat7
  • Speed: 1 Gbps (Cat5e), 10 Gbps (Cat6a), up to 100 Gbps (Cat8)
  • Purpose: Ethernet networking (LAN connections)
  • Max Distance: 100 meters (328 feet) typical
  • Found on: Computers, switches, routers, access points
  • Note: Larger than RJ11, won't fit in phone jacks

📞 RJ11 (Telephone Connector)

Use: Telephone lines and DSL modems

  • Pins: 4 or 6 pins (4P4C or 6P4C), only 2 used for phone
  • Cable Type: Standard telephone cable
  • Speed: Up to 56 Kbps (dial-up), 24 Mbps (DSL)
  • Purpose: Landline telephones, dial-up/DSL modems, fax
  • Found on: Phones, modems, wall phone jacks
  • Note: Smaller than RJ45, WILL physically fit in Ethernet port (but won't work)

💡 SFP (Small Form-Factor Pluggable)

Use: Fiber-optic network connections

  • Type: Hot-swappable transceiver module
  • Cable: Fiber-optic cable (glass/plastic fiber)
  • Speed: 1 Gbps (SFP), 10 Gbps (SFP+), 25/100 Gbps (newer)
  • Purpose: High-speed, long-distance data transmission
  • Max Distance: Up to 40–80 km (depending on fiber type)
  • Found on: Enterprise switches, routers, fiber patch panels
  • Advantages: Immune to EMI, very high bandwidth, long distance

✅ Concept Check

Question: Which connector is used for connecting computers to Ethernet networks using twisted-pair copper cables?

A) RJ11 (smaller phone connector)
B) RJ45 (8-pin Ethernet connector)
C) SFP (fiber-optic module)
D) USB-C (multipurpose connector)
Explanation: RJ45 is the standard Ethernet connector used for twisted-pair copper cables (Cat5e, Cat6, etc.). It has 8 pins and is found on computers, switches, and routers for LAN connections. RJ11 is for telephones. SFP is for fiber optics. USB-C can carry Ethernet via adapters but isn't the direct Ethernet connector.

Scenario — Graphics Card Upgrade
Graphics Card Upgrade Compatibility

A technician is upgrading the graphics card in a small business workstation. The existing monitor has a single video input: a wide, rectangular multi-pin connector that the manufacturer states supports both analog and digital video signals. The replacement graphics card must include a matching output port to avoid purchasing an adapter.

Which connector type must the new graphics card include to remain compatible with the existing monitor?
Hint: Focus on “supports both analog and digital video signals.”
📖 DVI — Transition Between Analog and Digital Eras

Explanation: DVI connectors were the industry's transition technology between analog VGA and modern digital-only standards. A monitor with a DVI input that supports both signal types requires a matching DVI port on the graphics card for an adapter-free connection. Without it, the technician would need a DVI-to-HDMI or DVI-to-DisplayPort adapter.


Function: Provides backward compatibility between analog and digital video equipment through a single connector.


In this scenario: The workstation's monitor requires DVI because it was manufactured before HDMI and DisplayPort became universal standards.


Key distinction: DVI bridges the CRT-to-LCD transition; HDMI and DisplayPort replaced it for purely digital connections on newer equipment.
